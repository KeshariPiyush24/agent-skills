# The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (No. 22 of 2023)

> **Provenance:** Agent-oriented paraphrase of Act No. 22 of 2023. Official text: [India Code — a2023-22.pdf](https://www.indiacode.nic.in/bitstream/123456789/22037/1/a2023-22.pdf). Commencement dates come from G.S.R. 843(E) (13 Nov 2025), not from condensed footnotes on secondary sites. This is **not** a substitute for the Gazette/India Code text. Verify against the official Act for legal decisions.

Received Presidential assent on 11 August 2023. An Act to provide for the processing of digital personal data in a manner that recognises both the right of individuals to protect their personal data and the need to process such personal data for lawful purposes. Brought into force in phases by notifications of 13 November 2025 (see `timelines-penalties.md`).

**Note:** The workspace Gazettes cover Rules 2025 + commencement/Board notifications. The Act itself is not among those PDFs; treat this file as a structured paraphrase pinned to India Code.

## Chapter I — Preliminary

### Section 1 — Short title and commencement

(1) This Act may be called the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023. (2) It comes into force on dates appointed by Central Government notification; different dates may be appointed for different provisions.

### Section 2 — Definitions

Key definitions (paraphrased faithfully). Letters (d), (e), (o) to (s), (v) and (w) are intentionally omitted here — if asked about one of those clauses, consult the official India Code text rather than inferring:

- **(a) Appellate Tribunal**: the Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT) under the TRAI Act, 1997.
- **(b) automated**: any digital process capable of operating automatically in response to instructions for processing data.
- **(c) Board**: the Data Protection Board of India established under Section 18.
- **(f) child**: an individual who has not completed the age of **eighteen years**.
- **(g) Consent Manager**: a person registered with the Board acting as a single point of contact enabling a Data Principal to give, manage, review and withdraw consent through an accessible, transparent and interoperable platform.
- **(h) data**: a representation of information, facts, concepts, opinions or instructions suitable for communication, interpretation or processing by humans or automated means.
- **(i) Data Fiduciary**: any person who alone or in conjunction with others **determines the purpose and means of processing** of personal data.
- **(j) Data Principal**: the individual to whom the personal data relates; includes parents/lawful guardian of a child, and the lawful guardian of a person with disability acting on her behalf.
- **(k) Data Processor**: any person who processes personal data **on behalf of** a Data Fiduciary.
- **(l) Data Protection Officer**: individual appointed by a Significant Data Fiduciary under Section 10(2)(a).
- **(m) digital office**: an office where proceedings from receipt to disposal are conducted in online/digital mode.
- **(n) digital personal data**: personal data in digital form.
- **(t) personal data**: any data about an individual who is **identifiable by or in relation to** such data.
- **(u) personal data breach**: any unauthorised processing of personal data or accidental disclosure, acquisition, sharing, use, alteration, destruction or loss of access to personal data, that compromises confidentiality, integrity or availability.
- **(x) processing**: a wholly or partly automated operation or set of operations on digital personal data, including collection, recording, organisation, structuring, storage, adaptation, retrieval, use, alignment or combination, indexing, sharing, disclosure by transmission, dissemination or otherwise making available, restriction, erasure or destruction.
- **(y) she**: refers to an individual irrespective of gender.
- **(z) Significant Data Fiduciary**: any Data Fiduciary or class notified by the Central Government under Section 10.
- **(za) specified purpose**: the purpose mentioned in the notice given by the Data Fiduciary to the Data Principal.
- **(zb) State**: as defined under Article 12 of the Constitution.

### Section 3 — Application of the Act

The Act applies to:

- (a) processing of digital personal data **within India** where the data is collected in digital form or in non-digital form and digitised subsequently;
- (b) processing of digital personal data **outside India**, if in connection with any activity related to offering goods or services to Data Principals within India.

It does **not** apply to: (c)(i) personal data processed by an individual for personal or domestic purposes; (c)(ii) personal data made publicly available by the Data Principal herself or by a person under a legal obligation to make it publicly available. (Illustration: an individual publicly blogging her personal data on social media — the Act does not apply.)

## Chapter II — Obligations of Data Fiduciary

### Section 4 — Grounds for processing personal data

(1) Personal data may be processed only in accordance with the Act and for a **lawful purpose**: (a) with the Data Principal's **consent**, or (b) for **certain legitimate uses** (Section 7). (2) "Lawful purpose" means any purpose not expressly forbidden by law.

### Section 5 — Notice

1. Every consent request must be accompanied or preceded by a notice informing the Data Principal of: (i) the personal data and the purpose of processing; (ii) how to exercise her rights under Sections 6(4) and 13; and (iii) how to complain to the Board — in the prescribed manner (see Rule 3).
2. Where consent was given **before commencement** of the Act, the Data Fiduciary must give such a notice as soon as reasonably practicable and may continue processing until consent is withdrawn.
3. The notice must be accessible in **English or any of the 22 languages in the Eighth Schedule to the Constitution**.

### Section 6 — Consent

1. Consent must be **free, specific, informed, unconditional and unambiguous with a clear affirmative action**, signifying agreement to processing for the specified purpose, and **limited to personal data necessary for that purpose**. (Illustration: a telemedicine app cannot rely on consent for phone-contact-list access, as that is not necessary for telemedicine.)
2. Any part of consent infringing the Act, rules or other law is invalid to that extent (e.g. a consent term waiving the right to complain to the Board is invalid).
3. Every consent request must be in clear and plain language, accessible in English or an Eighth Schedule language, and must provide contact details of the DPO or authorised person.
4. The Data Principal has the right to **withdraw consent at any time, with ease comparable to that with which it was given**.
5. Consequences of withdrawal are borne by the Data Principal; withdrawal does not affect legality of processing before withdrawal.
6. On withdrawal, the Data Fiduciary must, within a reasonable time, **cease processing and cause its Data Processors to cease**, unless processing without consent is otherwise required or authorised by law.
7. Consent may be given, managed, reviewed or withdrawn through a **Consent Manager**.
8. The Consent Manager is accountable to the Data Principal and acts on her behalf as prescribed.
9. Every Consent Manager must be **registered with the Board** subject to prescribed technical, operational, financial and other conditions (see Rule 4, First Schedule of the Rules).
10. Where consent is the basis of processing and a question arises in a proceeding, the **burden of proof is on the Data Fiduciary** to show notice was given and consent obtained.

### Section 7 — Certain legitimate uses

A Data Fiduciary may process personal data without consent for:

- (a) the specified purpose for which the Data Principal **voluntarily provided** her data and has not indicated non-consent (e.g. sending a payment receipt she requested);
- (b) the State providing or issuing prescribed subsidies, benefits, services, certificates, licences or permits (with prior consent for any of these, or from notified government databases), subject to prescribed standards;
- (c) performance by the State of any function under law, or in the interest of sovereignty and integrity of India or security of the State;
- (d) fulfilling any legal obligation to disclose information to the State;
- (e) compliance with any judgment, decree or order under Indian law, or foreign judgments/orders relating to contractual or civil claims;
- (f) responding to a **medical emergency** involving threat to life or immediate threat to health;
- (g) measures to provide medical treatment or health services during an **epidemic, outbreak of disease or other public-health threat**;
- (h) measures to ensure safety of, or provide assistance/services to, individuals during a **disaster or breakdown of public order**;
- (i) purposes of **employment** or safeguarding the employer from loss or liability (prevention of corporate espionage, confidentiality of trade secrets, IP, classified information) or provision of any service/benefit sought by an employee Data Principal.

### Section 8 — General obligations of Data Fiduciary

1. The Data Fiduciary is **responsible for compliance** for any processing by it or on its behalf by a Data Processor, irrespective of any contrary agreement or the Data Principal's failure to carry out her duties.
2. A Data Processor may be engaged **only under a valid contract**.
3. If personal data is likely to be used to make a decision affecting the Data Principal, or disclosed to another Data Fiduciary, the Data Fiduciary must ensure its **completeness, accuracy and consistency**.
4. Implement appropriate **technical and organisational measures** to ensure effective observance of the Act and rules.
5. Protect personal data in its possession or control (including with Data Processors) with **reasonable security safeguards** to prevent breach.
6. In the event of a personal data breach, give **the Board and each affected Data Principal** intimation in the prescribed form and manner (see Rule 7).
7. Unless retention is necessary for legal compliance: (a) **erase personal data** upon withdrawal of consent or as soon as it is reasonable to assume the specified purpose is no longer served, whichever is earlier; and (b) cause its Data Processor to erase data made available to it. (Illustrations: marketplace must delete data after a completed car sale; a bank retains KYC data for the statutory ten-year period.)
8. The purpose is **deemed no longer served** if the Data Principal neither approaches the Data Fiduciary for the specified purpose nor exercises her rights, for the prescribed time period (see Rule 8 and Third Schedule of the Rules).
9. **Publish business contact information** of the DPO (if applicable) or a person able to answer questions about processing (see Rule 9).
10. Establish an **effective grievance redressal mechanism**.
11. Clarification: a Data Principal is considered not to have approached the Data Fiduciary during any period she has not initiated contact, in person or by electronic/physical communication.

### Section 9 — Processing of personal data of children

1. Before processing personal data of a **child** or of a **person with disability who has a lawful guardian**, the Data Fiduciary must obtain **verifiable consent** of the parent or lawful guardian in the prescribed manner (see Rules 10 and 11).
2. A Data Fiduciary shall not undertake processing **likely to cause any detrimental effect on the well-being of a child**.
3. A Data Fiduciary shall not undertake **tracking or behavioural monitoring of children or targeted advertising directed at children**.
4. Sub-sections (1) and (3) do not apply to prescribed classes of Data Fiduciaries or purposes, subject to prescribed conditions (see Rule 12 and Fourth Schedule of the Rules — healthcare, education, child safety, etc.).
5. The Central Government may notify a lower age threshold for a Data Fiduciary that processes children's data in a verifiably safe manner, exempting it from Sections 9(1)/(3) obligations above that age.

### Section 10 — Additional obligations of Significant Data Fiduciary

1. The Central Government may notify any Data Fiduciary or class as **Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF)** based on factors including: volume and sensitivity of personal data processed; risk to Data Principal rights; potential impact on sovereignty and integrity of India; risk to electoral democracy; security of the State; public order.
2. The SDF shall:
   - (a) appoint a **Data Protection Officer** who represents the SDF, is **based in India**, is responsible to the board of directors or similar governing body, and is the contact point for grievance redressal;
   - (b) appoint an **independent data auditor** to evaluate compliance;
   - (c) undertake the following other measures: (i) **periodic Data Protection Impact Assessment** — a process comprising a description of Data Principal rights and processing purposes, assessment and management of risks to those rights, and other prescribed matters; (ii) **periodic audit**; (iii) other prescribed measures (see Rule 13).

## Chapter III — Rights and Duties of Data Principal

### Section 11 — Right to access information about personal data

1. The Data Principal may obtain from a Data Fiduciary to whom she gave consent (including Section 7(a) consent): (a) a **summary of the personal data being processed and the processing activities**; (b) the **identities of all other Data Fiduciaries and Data Processors with whom the data has been shared**, with a description of the data shared; and (c) any other prescribed information.
2. Clauses (b)/(c) do not apply to sharing with a Data Fiduciary authorised by law to obtain the data, pursuant to a written request for prevention/detection/investigation of offences or cyber incidents, or prosecution/punishment of offences.

### Section 12 — Right to correction and erasure of personal data

1. Right to **correction, completion, updating and erasure** of personal data processed with her consent (including Section 7(a)), in accordance with applicable law.
2. On a request, the Data Fiduciary shall correct inaccurate or misleading data, complete incomplete data, and update the data.
3. On an erasure request (in the prescribed manner), the Data Fiduciary shall **erase** the personal data unless retention is necessary for the specified purpose or legal compliance.

### Section 13 — Right of grievance redressal

1. Right to **readily available means of grievance redressal** provided by a Data Fiduciary or Consent Manager for any act or omission regarding obligations or the exercise of rights.
2. The Data Fiduciary or Consent Manager must respond within the prescribed period (Rule 14(3): published period not exceeding **ninety days**).
3. The Data Principal must **exhaust the grievance redressal opportunity before approaching the Board**.

### Section 14 — Right to nominate

The Data Principal may nominate, in the prescribed manner, any other individual to exercise her rights in the event of her **death or incapacity** (unsoundness of mind or infirmity of body).

### Section 15 — Duties of Data Principal

The Data Principal shall: (a) comply with applicable laws when exercising rights; (b) not impersonate another person when providing personal data; (c) not suppress material information when providing personal data for documents, unique identifiers, or proofs of identity/address issued by the State; (d) not register a **false or frivolous grievance or complaint**; (e) furnish only verifiably authentic information when exercising the right to correction or erasure. (Breach of duties is penalised — see the Schedule, up to Rs 10,000.)

## Chapter IV — Special Provisions

### Section 16 — Processing of personal data outside India

1. The Central Government may, by notification, **restrict transfer** of personal data to notified countries or territories outside India (a "negative list" approach — transfers are permitted by default unless restricted; see also Rule 15).
2. This does not restrict any Indian law providing a **higher degree of protection or restriction** on cross-border transfer (e.g. sectoral rules such as RBI data-localisation directions continue to apply).

### Section 17 — Exemptions

1. Chapter II (except Sections 8(1) and 8(5)), Chapter III and Section 16 do not apply where processing is: (a) necessary for enforcing any legal right or claim; (b) by courts/tribunals or judicial/quasi-judicial/regulatory/supervisory bodies for their functions; (c) in the interest of prevention, detection, investigation or prosecution of any offence or contravention; (d) of Data Principals **outside India** pursuant to a contract with a person outside India by a person based in India (outsourcing exemption); (e) necessary for court-approved schemes of compromise/arrangement/merger/amalgamation/demerger; (f) for ascertaining financial information, assets and liabilities of a **loan defaulter**, per applicable disclosure laws.
2. The Act does not apply to processing: (a) by **notified State instrumentalities** in the interests of sovereignty and integrity of India, security of the State, friendly foreign relations, public order or preventing incitement to cognizable offences; (b) necessary for **research, archiving or statistical purposes** if not used for decisions specific to a Data Principal and per prescribed standards (see Rule 16 and Second Schedule of the Rules).
3. The Central Government may notify certain Data Fiduciaries or classes (**including startups**) exempt from Section 5, Sections 8(3) and 8(7), and Sections 10 and 11.
4. For State processing: Sections 8(7), 12(3) — and, where no decision affecting the Data Principal is made, 12(2) — do not apply.
5. The Central Government may, within five years of commencement, declare any provision inapplicable to any Data Fiduciary or class for a specified period.

## Chapter V — Data Protection Board of India

- **Section 18**: Establishment of the Board as a body corporate; headquarters as notified (established 13 Nov 2025; HQ in the National Capital Region).
- **Section 19**: Composition — a Chairperson and Members as notified (four Members notified); appointed as prescribed; persons of ability, integrity and standing with knowledge in data governance, law, ICT, digital economy, dispute resolution etc.; at least one law expert.
- **Section 20**: Salary and terms as prescribed, not variable to disadvantage after appointment; **term of two years**, eligible for re-appointment.
- **Section 21**: Disqualifications (insolvency, conviction involving moral turpitude, incapacity, prejudicial financial interest, abuse of position); removal only after opportunity of being heard.
- **Section 22**: Resignation and vacancies; one-year post-office employment restriction (approval required; disclosure of employment with Data Fiduciaries proceeded against).
- **Section 23**: Board meeting procedure as prescribed; acts not invalid for vacancy/defect/irregularity; senior-most Member acts when Chairperson unavailable.
- **Section 24**: Officers and employees appointed with Central Government approval.
- **Section 25**: Members and officers are public servants (Section 21, IPC).
- **Section 26**: Powers of Chairperson — superintendence, authorising scrutiny of complaints, allocating proceedings among Members.

## Chapter VI — Powers, Functions and Procedure of the Board

### Section 27 — Powers and functions of the Board

1. The Board shall: (a) on breach intimation under Section 8(6), direct **urgent remedial or mitigation measures**, inquire and impose penalty; (b) on a Data Principal's complaint (breach of data or obligations or rights), or a government reference, or court direction, inquire and impose penalty; (c) on complaint about a Consent Manager, inquire and impose penalty; (d) on intimation of breach of Consent Manager registration conditions, inquire and impose penalty; (e) on Central Government reference about intermediary breach of Section 37(2), inquire and impose penalty.
2. The Board may issue binding directions after hearing and recorded reasons.
3. The Board may modify, suspend, withdraw or cancel directions.

### Section 28 — Procedure

The Board functions as an independent **digital office** (digital-by-design complaints, hearings, decisions). It determines whether sufficient grounds exist, may close proceedings or inquire, follows natural justice, has civil-court powers (summoning, evidence on affidavit, discovery, inspection), must not disrupt day-to-day functioning or take equipment into custody, may requisition police/government officers, may issue interim orders after hearing, and on completion either closes proceedings or proceeds under Section 33. It may warn or impose costs for **false or frivolous complaints**.

## Chapter VII — Appeal and Alternate Dispute Resolution

- **Section 29**: Appeal to the **Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT)** within **sixty days** of receipt of the Board's order (extendable for sufficient cause); Tribunal endeavours to dispose within six months; functions as a digital office; further appeals per Section 18 of the TRAI Act (to the Supreme Court).
- **Section 30**: Tribunal orders executable as a civil-court decree.
- **Section 31**: The Board may direct **mediation** where a complaint may be resolved by it.
- **Section 32**: The Board may accept a **voluntary undertaking** (action, abstention, publicising); acceptance bars proceedings on its contents; breach of the undertaking is deemed breach of the Act and may lead to Section 33 penalties.

## Chapter VIII — Penalties and Adjudication

### Section 33 — Penalties

1. If, on conclusion of an inquiry, the Board determines a **significant breach**, it may impose the monetary penalty specified in the Schedule after hearing the person.
2. Factors: nature, gravity and duration of breach; type and nature of personal data affected; repetitiveness; gain realised or loss avoided; mitigation actions and their timeliness/effectiveness; proportionality and deterrent effect; likely impact of the penalty on the person.

### Section 34

All penalty sums are credited to the Consolidated Fund of India.

## Chapter IX — Miscellaneous

- **Section 35**: Protection of action taken in good faith (Government, Board, members, officers).
- **Section 36**: Central Government may call for information from the Board, any Data Fiduciary or intermediary (see Rule 23).
- **Section 37**: On a Board reference (penalties imposed in **two or more instances**, plus advice in the general public interest), the Central Government may, after hearing the Data Fiduciary, direct **blocking of public access** to information/computer resources enabling the Data Fiduciary to offer goods or services in India. Intermediaries are bound to comply.
- **Section 38**: The Act is in addition to, not in derogation of, other laws; in conflict, **this Act prevails** to the extent of the conflict.
- **Section 39**: Civil courts barred from entertaining suits on matters within the Board's power; no injunctions against actions under the Act.
- **Section 40**: Central Government's rule-making power (the DPDP Rules, 2025 were made under this section).
- **Section 41**: Rules and certain notifications to be laid before Parliament.
- **Section 42**: Power to amend the Schedule (penalties may not be increased to more than **twice** the originally enacted amounts).
- **Section 43**: Power to remove difficulties (within three years of commencement).
- **Section 44**: Consequential amendments — TRAI Act, 1997 (TDSAT jurisdiction); **IT Act, 2000: Section 43A omitted** (old compensation regime for negligent handling of sensitive personal data), Section 81 proviso and Section 87(2)(ob) amended; **RTI Act, 2005: Section 8(1)(j) substituted** ("information which relates to personal information" exempt from disclosure).
